Basic Pesto? More Like Basic Privilege.

When presented with a task which is described with the word “basic”, one would usually assume that the task can be easily achieved by anyone through following the same instructions. However, the current American society seems to have lost grasp of the true meaning of “basic”. The Basic Pesto recipe, as seen on the New York Times cooking website, hints at the impossibility of achieving the American dream through the indirect references to privilege. Flawed assumptions of what constitutes “basic” and “accessible” made by Florence Fabricant, the author of the recipe, speaks of the inherent ignorance in our current day world towards issues regarding inequality.

Firstly, throughout the history of North America, communities have been divided due to systemic discrimination and segregation in a similar manner to the fresh basil that the recipe calls for. However, unlike the metaphorical divide of groups of people, the leaves and the stem of the basil are quite literally physically separated, highlighting the lack of symbiotic relationships between individuals. Much like how communities and industries rely on each other in order to create a thriving society, a basil plant cannot exist without the joint efforts of the basil leaves and stems. However, while the basil leaves become the star of the pesto show, the stems' tastes are quite literally discriminated against, and are discarded without any care nor consideration for their contributions. The leaves are, quite literally, taking credit for work that they have not done simply because they have the privilege of having a “superior taste” according to societal norms. Division (similar to the division of basil leaves in the recipe) is a recurring issue throughout the history of the world. For example, the Berlin Wall, which was built in 1961, segregated communities and promoted gentrification within the city of Berlin. These communities which were segregated suffered the effects of gentrification, which erased a large part of their culture (Wikipedia). From this, one can conclude that many underappreciated people suffer. An example of this is Basil from The Picture of Dorian Gray by Oscar Wilde, who, similar to the basil in the recipe, was stabbed to death (SparkNotes). Through instructing the reader to separate parts of basil, the author highlights the reliance yet ignorance society has on the “basil stems” of the world, which is ultimately going to lead to disaster due to human nature.

Secondly, human nature is the root of a number of social constructs. In Fabricant's Basic Pesto, the author calls for her readers to “dribble in the oil”. What oil is dribbled? A singular human homosapien individual may ask. When one closely reads the recipe, they are hit in the face by the words “extra-virgin” (Fabricant). How does one truly achieve the state of extra-virginism? This impossible state of being, created by the social constructs which are caused by human nature, is something that not even a League of Legends player can achieve. The concept of virginity can be traced back to the Bible, when the Virgin Mary gave birth to Jesus Christ, which can be directly translated to “Oily Josh” (Bible.com, Matt. 1.18). This extra virgin olive oil is, in reality, a reference to the birth of “Oily Josh” by the Virgin Mary. This can be seen in how Mary is a virgin, and Jesus Christ's name directly translates to “Oily Josh”, according to Reddit user Big_Boss_Bubba. This preference, or rather idealization, of virginity, can be seen as conforming to archaic societal standards. Given that the recipe was written in the 21st century, this piece by Fabricant can be directly inferred to be against the feminist movements in the author's time.

Thirdly, when listing the ingredients for the recipe, Fabricant never once mentions the requirement of a food processor or refrigerator. This, in itself, is a juxtaposition to the word “basic” in the recipe's title, for food processors and refrigerators are items of luxury for many who struggle in the current society that juxtaposes wealth and poverty. This juxtaposition clearly exemplifies an availability heuristic, in which abricant is surrounded by people who are privileged enough to afford food processors and refrigerators, thus believing that it is the norm. This juxtaposition juxtaposed with the previous juxtapositions clearly juxtaposizes the juxtapositions which are juxtaposed within this recipe. Privilege makes the pesto taste of the bitter juxtaposition between the juxtaposed and the unjuxtaposed.

Fourthly, Fabricant's call for cheese in the pesto exposes her as a part of the infamous Milk Conspiracy. The Milk Conspiracy states that ones who are not lactose intolerant promote the milk market in order to eliminate those who are considered as “genetically inferior” (people who are lactose intolerant) (Hicks). As an immigrant Asian female woman who loves my culture and immigrated through a process of immigration, I strongly feel for my peers who are lactose intolerant and I aspire to pursue academia in order to physically, mentally, psychologically, financially, and spiritually support my cultural community, many of which suffer from the oppressive intolerance of lactose. I believe that the diverse community at the University of British Columbia will help me achieve that goal.

To conclude, the taste of pesto might be horrid, but the message behind Basic Pesto by Florence Fabricant is beyond the aforementioned horridness. It can be clearly seen that as a community, individuals should come together and face the oppression that is pesto propaganda, or as I like to call it, pestopaganda.
